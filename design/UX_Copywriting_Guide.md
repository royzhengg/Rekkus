# UX Copywriting: A Practical Guide

> **Objective:** A consolidated reference for writing clear, consistent, and effective UX copy — covering tone of voice, punctuation, grammar, and UI-specific standards.

---

## Part 1: Core Philosophy & Guiding Principles

### 1.1 What is UX Writing?

UX Writing, or **"Microcopy"**, refers to the small words and phrases within a product interface that guide users through it. Its primary purpose is to help the user — making interfaces easy to use and understand.

---

### 1.2 Goals of Good UX Copy

| Goal | Description |
|---|---|
| **Just make things easy** | Users should easily use and understand the interface with minimal assistance. |
| **Copy for everyone** | Interfaces should be understandable and usable by people with disabilities and across various contexts and platforms. |
| **Don't make me think** | Users should accomplish tasks quickly without excessive mental effort. |
| **A repeatable experience** | Users should enjoy using the interface and want to return to it. |

---

### 1.3 How to Ensure Good UX Copy

- **Test it** — Frequent usability testing is the most effective method.
- **Analyse user feedback** — Review support tickets and help requests to identify and target problem areas.

---

### 1.4 Finding Your Voice

Your product's language and tone should bring your brand to life. A strong UX voice is typically:

- **Human and direct** — it speaks like a person, not a manual.
- **Consistent** — it sounds the same whether you're onboarding a new user or showing an error message.
- **Purposeful** — every word earns its place.

Whatever your specific brand voice, it should be documented and shared across your team so copy stays consistent at every touchpoint.

---

### 1.5 Three Golden Rules for Tone of Voice

These rules apply regardless of industry or product type.

> ### 1. ✂️ Cut the fluff
> Keep it real. Tell it like it is. Use short, simple, to-the-point language that feels relatable and human.

> ### 2. 🙌 Serve the user
> Acknowledge what users are trying to do and show them how to succeed. Every piece of copy should help them move forward.

> ### 3. 🚀 Leave them better off
> Users should feel clearer, more confident, and more capable after reading your copy — never confused, patronised, or anxious.

## Part 2: The 5 Principles of Great UX Writing

### 2.1 Usable

**Definition:** Words that improve or enable the functionality of a product.

**Goal:** Makes it easy for people to use the product without thinking too much.

**In practice:** Use clear, simple, to-the-point language. If a user has to re-read something to understand it, rewrite it.

---

### 2.2 Helpful

**Definition:** Goes beyond usability to offer useful information the user might not have known they needed.

**Example:** A search bar saying *"Search for flights by city or airport"* instead of just *"Search"*.

**In practice:** Anticipate questions before they're asked. Proactively surface the right information at the right moment.

---

### 2.3 Accessible

**Definition:** The interface should be for everyone, including people with disabilities.

**Benefit:** Accessible copy benefits all users — it reduces cognitive load and makes the interface easier for everyone to use.

**In practice:** Avoid jargon, idioms, or culturally specific references that may exclude people. Write for a broad audience by default.

---

### 2.4 Clear

**Definition:** UX copy prioritises clarity and user assistance over punchy or clever marketing copy.

**In practice:** When in doubt, choose the clearer option over the cleverer one. Use short, sharp sentences. One idea per sentence.

---

### 2.5 Branded

**Definition:** UX copy should feel consistent with your brand — in look, feel, and language — across every touchpoint.

**In practice:** Develop and maintain a content style guide that defines your brand voice, tone, editorial style, and messaging. Make sure everyone writing copy has access to it.

#### Core brand traits to define for your product

Most strong product brands embody traits across three dimensions. Use this as a starting framework:

| Dimension | What it means | What it doesn't mean |
|---|---|---|
| **Empowering** | You're a partner, not a gatekeeper. Help users feel capable and confident. | Condescending, oversimplifying, or doing the thinking for them. |
| **Transformative** | You offer something genuinely better. Be a forward-thinker. | Unrealistic promises, toxic positivity, or sugar-coating problems. |
| **Distinctive** | You have a point of view. Don't sound like every other product. | Trying too hard, being contrarian for its own sake, or excluding users. |

## Part 3: Punctuation and Grammar Standards

### 3.1 Capitalisation

**General rule:** Use **minimal capitalisation** for all text unless an exception applies.

**Minimal capitalisation defined:** Only capitalise proper nouns and the first word in a sentence or standalone phrase of two or more words.

> ✅ `Save and continue` &nbsp;&nbsp; ✅ `More information` &nbsp;&nbsp; ✅ `Confirm your details`

#### What counts as a proper noun?

- Names of people
- Names of institutions (e.g., Australian Tax Office, HMRC)
- Names of companies (e.g., Stripe, Xero)
- Names of products or brands (e.g., Salesforce, Slack)
- Names of places

> ⚠️ **Note:** Product features and UI elements are generally **not** proper nouns.
> ✅ `Email notifications` &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ `Email Notifications`

#### Minimal capitalisation applies to:

- Sidebar menu items
- Dashboard tabs
- Headings
- Wizard titles
- Modal titles
- Buttons
- Drop-down menu items

#### Industry terminology

Follow the capitalisation conventions used by the relevant regulatory or industry body. When in doubt, default to minimal capitalisation.

| ✅ Correct | ❌ Incorrect |
|---|---|
| `terms and conditions` | `Terms and Conditions` |
| `two-factor authentication` | `Two-Factor Authentication` |
| `privacy policy` | `Privacy Policy` |

> **Exception:** Some industry terms have an established capitalised form that users expect. Always check against the relevant authority or common product conventions.

---

### 3.1.1 Exceptions to Minimal Capitalisation

| Exception | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **a. User-entered text** | Display text exactly as the user entered it. | `BOB'S GARDENING SERVICES` |
| **b. Page titles (h2 tags)** | Use **maximal capitalisation** — capitalise the first word and every content word; do not capitalise function words. | `Getting Started With Your Account` |
| **c. Navigation section titles** | Use **full caps** for section groupings; use minimal caps for individual items within them. | Section: `MY ACCOUNT` / Item: `Account details` |
| **d. Status labels** | Use **full caps**. | `ACTIVE`, `PENDING`, `INCOMPLETE` |
| **e. Acronyms/initialisations** | Use **full caps** when created from the first letter of each word. | `API`, `GST`, `URL` |
| **f. The abbreviation "ID"** | Always `ID`, never `Id` or `id`. | `User ID`, `Order ID` |

---

### 3.1.2 Measurement Units

Use lower case for all measurement units.

> ✅ `hrs` &nbsp;&nbsp; ✅ `kg` &nbsp;&nbsp; ✅ `mb` &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ `Hrs`

---

### 3.2 Spelling and Syntax

**Choose a spelling standard and stick to it** — British English, American English, or the standard appropriate to your primary market. Set it as the default across your tools and document it in your style guide.

> **Example (British vs. American):** `Enrolment` vs. `Enrollment` · `Colour` vs. `Color` · `Organise` vs. `Organize`

---

#### Phrasal verbs vs. phrasal nouns

This is one of the most common sources of inconsistency in product copy.

**Quick test:** Try changing the tense. If it sounds wrong (e.g., *loginned*, *setupped*), it's a noun — write it as a compound.

| Type | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Phrasal verb** | Keep verb and particle **separate** when particle follows the verb. | `log in`, `set up`, `sign out` |
| **Phrasal verb** | Compound when particle precedes the verb. | `download`, `upload`, `override` |
| **Phrasal noun** | Write as a **compound word**. | `login`, `setup`, `dropdown` |

---

### 3.3 Common Terms Quick Reference

Use this as a starting point and expand it for your own product vocabulary.

| Term | Correct usage | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| `cannot` | `cannot` | Not `can not` |
| `drop-down` | `drop-down` (noun/adj), `drop down` (verb) | Hyphenated noun |
| `email` | `email` | Not `e-mail` |
| `ID` | `ID` | Not `Id` or `id` |
| `log in` | `log in` (verb) | e.g., *Log in to your account* |
| `login` | `login` (noun/adj) | e.g., *Enter your login details* |
| `pop-up` | `pop-up` (noun/adj), `pop up` (verb) | Hyphenated noun |
| `set up` | `set up` (verb) | e.g., *Set up your profile* |
| `setup` | `setup` (noun) | e.g., *Complete the setup* |
| `sign in` / `sign out` | `sign in`, `sign out` (verbs) | Separate; use consistently over `log in` or vice versa — not both |
| `username` | `username` | One word |

> 📝 **Tip:** Maintain your own product-specific quick reference. The most important thing isn't which convention you choose — it's that you choose one and apply it consistently.

## Part 4: Specific UI Elements & Content Types

### 4.1 Using Links

**Purpose:** Indicating navigation within or directly following a sentence.

#### ✅ Do this

- Write link text that describes the **action or destination**.
  - ✅ `Reset your password`
  - ✅ `View the activity log`
  - ✅ `Read the privacy policy`
- Open **internal links** in the same tab.
- Open **external links** (support docs, partner pages, third-party sites) in a new tab.

#### ❌ Avoid this

- Vague anchor text like `click here`, `this link`, `here`, or `go here`.
  - ❌ `Click here to see more details` → ✅ `View details`
  - ❌ `Use this form to apply` → ✅ `Complete the application form`
- Unintuitive references like `see the link above` — the link's purpose should be self-evident.
- Using links for actions like saving or cancelling — use **buttons** for those instead.

---

### 4.2 Using Information Labels (i-icon)

**Purpose:** Provide contextual information to help users understand a function, setting, or field.

#### Usage rules

- Use **sparingly** — not on every field or label.
- Only for genuinely **additional** information that can't be surfaced elsewhere.
- **Do not use for:**
  - Explaining complex logic (simplify the logic instead)
  - Warnings or errors (use validation components)

#### Best practices

| Setting | Rule |
|---|---|
| **Icon** | Use a standard info `i` icon — not `?` or `!` |
| **Interaction** | Show a popover on hover and on click |
| **Visibility** | Keep popover visible while hovering + ~500ms after mouse moves away |
| **Text length** | Maximum 2–3 sentences; link to a support article for anything longer |

---

### 4.3 Tone of Voice by Content Type

Tone isn't one-size-fits-all. The same brand voice should flex depending on where the copy appears.

| Content type | Sounds like | Not like |
|---|---|---|
| **CTAs** | Active, direct, specific. Unexpected phrasing can be memorable. | Generic: *"Learn more"*, *"Find out more"*, *"Submit"* |
| **Emails** | Human, warm, like a message from a knowledgeable colleague. Lead with value. | Dense walls of text, corporate language, impersonal openers. |
| **Onboarding** | Encouraging, clear, step-by-step. Celebrate progress. | Overwhelming, assumption-heavy, or rushed. |
| **Error messages** | Calm, specific, constructive. Tell the user what happened and what to do next. | Blame-y, vague (*"Something went wrong"*), or overly technical. |
| **Empty states** | Helpful and inviting. Prompt the next action. | Blank, cold, or unhelpful. |
| **Short-form docs (1–2 pages)** | Simple, direct, educational. Information straight up, minimal storytelling. | Corporate padding, unnecessary preamble, or filler. |
| **Long-form content** | Engaging, storytelling-led, takes the reader on a journey. | Tedious, overly long, or repetitive. |
| **Tooltips & helper text** | Concise, specific, practical. | Repeating what the label already says. |

---

### 4.4 General Tone Tips

- **Lean into the unexpected** — a surprising turn of phrase is more memorable than a safe one.
- **Bring warmth to every interaction** — even error pages and empty states can have a human touch.
- **Less shouting, more confidence** — short, sharp sentences land harder than exclamation marks.
- **Avoid filler openers** — *"In today's fast-paced world..."* or *"Did you know?"* add nothing. Get to the point.
- **Emojis, icons, and illustrations** can reinforce tone when used intentionally — don't overdo it.

## Part 4.5: Settings — Appearance Copy

### Theme selector

The Settings → Appearance row uses a 3-pill segmented control. Canonical labels (do not alter casing or wording):

| Option     | Label     | Notes                                    |
| ---------- | --------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Light mode | Light     | Single word, title case                  |
| Dark mode  | Dark      | Single word, title case                  |
| Follow OS  | Follow OS | Two words; "OS" always fully capitalised |

Use "Follow OS" — not "System", "Auto", or "Automatic" — to make the source of the setting explicit to the user.

## Part 5: Building Your Own Terminology Guide

Every product has words that need consistent treatment. Here's how to build and maintain one.

### 5.1 Why it matters

Inconsistent terminology erodes trust. If your app says `sign in` on the homepage and `log in` on the modal, users notice — even if they can't articulate why. Consistency signals polish and professionalism.

### 5.2 What to include

Your terminology guide should document:

- **Product-specific terms** — names of features, modules, or concepts unique to your product.
- **Industry terms** — any regulated or technical language you use, with the correct casing and form.
- **Compound vs. separated forms** — for any phrasal verbs or nouns your product uses frequently.
- **Terms to avoid** — internal jargon, deprecated names, or confusing synonyms.

### 5.3 How to introduce new terminology

When introducing a new term or acronym:

1. **Spell it out in full** on first use.
2. **Follow with the short form** in parentheses if you'll use it repeatedly.
3. **Use the short form** consistently thereafter.

> **Example:** *"You can manage this through the Content Management System (CMS). The CMS also allows you to..."*

### 5.3a Rekkus canonical term list

| Concept | Use | Do not use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Food establishment | **place** | restaurant, location, venue, spot |
| User-created save grouping | **collection** | list, folder |
| Save action / saved tab | **save / saved** | bookmark, favourite |
| User-generated content | **post** | review, entry |
| Save a place | **Save** | Bookmark |
| Remove saved place | **Unsave** | Unbookmark, remove bookmark |

### 5.4 Tips for maintaining consistency

- Store your terminology guide somewhere the whole team can access and contribute to it.
- Review it whenever a product or feature is renamed.
- Include it in onboarding for new designers, writers, and developers.
- Run periodic audits of live copy to catch drift.

## Quick Checklist: Before You Ship Copy

Use this as a final review before any copy goes live.

- [ ] Is every word earning its place? Cut anything that doesn't help the user.
- [ ] Is the capitalisation consistent with the standards in Part 3?
- [ ] Are phrasal verbs and nouns treated correctly and consistently?
- [ ] Does the tone match the content type (Part 4.3)?
- [ ] Are links descriptive and meaningful — no "click here"?
- [ ] Are error messages specific, calm, and actionable?
- [ ] Does the copy work for someone using a screen reader or assistive technology?
- [ ] Is it consistent with your terminology guide?
- [ ] Have you tested it (or read it aloud) to check it sounds natural?

## Create-Flow Conventions (2026-06-01)

- **Location fields on creation flows use `"Where did you eat?"` not `"Search for a restaurant…"`** — directive copy signals the field's purpose; search-style copy undersells the metadata value.
- **Single primary media CTA → action sheet is canonical for media source selection.** Do not render Camera and Library as two parallel buttons — use one "Add photos" button that opens a `RekkusActionSheet` with Camera and Photo library as options. Camera first (primary action), Photo library second.
- **Nearby count sub-label gives passive location confidence.** When nearby places are loaded and the location field is unfocused and empty, show `"N place(s) near you"` below the field. This signals platform context before the user types. Hide once focused or once a place is confirmed.
- **Character counters appear only while focused.** A counter at rest (`0/100`) exposes implementation detail without value. Show it only when the user is actively typing. Apply `accessibilityLiveRegion="polite"` so screen readers announce the value on change.
